Proceratosaurus is a genus of carnivorous theropod dinosaur that lived during the Middle Jurassic period (Bathonian stage), about 166 to 164 million years ago. A small to medium-sized predator, it is known from a single, nearly complete skull discovered in the Forest Marble Formation of Gloucestershire, England. This skull shows a small, bony nasal horn, which was originally thought to be the beginning of a larger crest like that of Ceratosaurus, giving the dinosaur its name meaning "before Ceratosaurus".

Discovery

The holotype and only known specimen of Proceratosaurus (NHM R 4860) was discovered in the Forest Marble Formation of Minchinhampton, Gloucestershire, England. It consists of a nearly complete skull that measures approximately 30.2 centimeters (11.9 inches) in length. The specimen was originally described by Arthur Smith Woodward in 1910 as a new species of Megalosaurus, M. bradleyi, named after its collector F. Lewis Bradley.

Diagram of the skull with bones color-coded

In 1926, German paleontologist Friedrich von Huene recognized that it was not a species of Megalosaurus and placed it in its own genus, Proceratosaurus, meaning "before Ceratosaurus", due to the nasal horn that he interpreted as the precursor to the larger crest of Ceratosaurus. For much of the 20th century, Proceratosaurus was considered a basal relative of Ceratosaurus within the ceratosaur family. However, phylogenetic studies in the 21st century have shown it to be a coelurosaur, specifically one of the earliest known members of the tyrannosauroid lineage.

Description

Proceratosaurus was a small to medium-sized theropod. The holotype skull is 30.2 cm (11.9 in) long, suggesting a total body length of about 3 meters (9.8 feet). Some estimates suggest it may have reached up to 4 meters (13 feet) in length.

The most distinctive feature of Proceratosaurus is the nasal horn on the top of its snout, which was formed by fused nasal bones. This horn was relatively small and blade-like, unlike the larger, rugose horns of Ceratosaurus. The skull was long and low, with large fenestrae (openings) to reduce weight. It had sharp, recurved teeth typical of theropod dinosaurs, with serrations on both the anterior and posterior edges.

Size comparison with a human

The antorbital fenestra (the opening in front of the eye) was large and housed a smaller opening within it, the maxillary fenestra. This characteristic is shared with other proceratosaurids and tyrannosauroids. The lower jaw was slender and contained numerous teeth. Like other theropods, Proceratosaurus was bipedal, with short arms and longer, powerful hind limbs.

Classification

For much of the 20th century, Proceratosaurus was classified as a basal ceratosaur, closely related to Ceratosaurus due to the presence of a nasal horn. However, a detailed redescription of the holotype skull by Oliver Rauhut and colleagues in 2010 found that it shared many features with tyrannosauroids.

Phylogenetic analysis placed Proceratosaurus as a basal member of the Proceratosauridae family within Tyrannosauroidea. It is closely related to other proceratosaurids such as Guanlong, Kileskus, and Sinotyrannus. These dinosaurs are characterized by cranial ornamentation (such as crests or horns) and shared skeletal features.

Paleobiology

As a medium-sized theropod, Proceratosaurus was likely an active predator. Its sharp, serrated teeth suggest it preyed on other vertebrates, possibly including small dinosaurs, mammals, lizards, and other contemporary fauna.

The function of the nasal horn is uncertain. It may have been used for display purposes, such as species recognition or sexual display, rather than for combat. Similar structures in modern animals often serve such purposes.

Studies of braincase morphology suggest that Proceratosaurus had well-developed senses of smell, vision, and hearing, which would have been advantageous for a predator. Its brain was relatively large for a dinosaur of its size, suggesting potentially complex behavior.

Paleoecology

Proceratosaurus lived during the Middle Jurassic period (Bathonian stage), approximately 166 to 164 million years ago. Its remains were found in the Forest Marble Formation, which represents a shallow marine environment with nearby islands or coastal areas.

The ecosystem included various dinosaurs such as the sauropod Cetiosaurus, the stegosaur Lexovisaurus, and the early ankylosaur Sarcolestes. Other contemporary animals included crocodilians, pterosaurs, turtles, and early mammals.

As one of the larger predators in its environment, Proceratosaurus likely occupied an important ecological niche, possibly preying on smaller dinosaurs and other vertebrates.
